Paramount's [anticipated acquisition](https://www.paramount.com/press/paramount-to-acquire-warner-bros-discovery-to-form-next-generation-global-media-and-entertainment-company) of Warner Bros. got me thinking. It represents the largest move to consolidate the streaming platforms yet.

We've already seen a widening gulf in how movies are created over the years. There are action movies and Oscar bait in theaters, while a handful of tv shows thrive on streaming.

Less platforms though, means less choice. As someone who likes variety and things to choose from, the consolidating market suggests an attack on that choice set.

## Who Am I

I'm not someone who works in the industry, has any expertise in the matter, or any other sort of person you'd consider qualified to write about something like this. Instead, I am a Software Engineer who watches a lot of movies, tv shows, and standup who would like to complain to the open internet and throw a dart in the direction of a solution.

## The Illusion of Choice

When talking through how the modern media landscape limits what you watch, the easy retort is that "you can more readily watch anything today than ever before in history". This is a fact. It is deeply misleading though.

Today we live in a period of illusion of choice with most things, and especially media. We have access to more things than ever before, but have been conditioned and structurally directed towards narrowing that set of choices.

### Tonight I'm gonna watch something!

Let's talk through a given night... You sit down after dinner around the TV and you're going to choose something to watch. You open a streaming app and start scrolling for something that looks good. You scroll for about 5 minutes, then land on rewatching something or clicking on something attention grabbing.

### Why it's an illusion

This story is universal and perfectly descriptive of the Illusion of Choice we have with media today.

Let's say you have 3 streaming services; Hooli, Nestflix, and Tantamount+. Each has 5k titles, 3k movies + 2k tv shows ([approximation source](https://www.justwatch.com/us/provider/netflix)).

Now, when you first sat down to watch, you chose an app that cuts your options from 15k to 5k. Next you scrolled for 5 minutes. So now you've cut your options from 5k to about 50 titles, depending on how fast you scroll and read. 50 titles out of 15k you have access to represents 0.33% of your total access set. When you log back on the next day, you repeat the same thing, except now, Nestflix sees you really like Will Ferrell after you watched Kicking & Screaming last night, so from now till the end of time 10/50 of the options you see will be Will Ferrell films.

I'm exaggerating a bit, but it does not take a large imagination to see how small of a share of what we have to choose from, we actually see on any given night

## What do I care most about as a viewer?

Personally, I like variety, surprise, and a really good story. Some nights I want an easy watch with a good laugh. Other nights I want a serious flick that keeps me on the edge of my seat. Or a documentary that teaches me about a chapter I didn't read in history class. A nature doc that takes me to a place I could never possibly go. And so on...

Other people want to watch the thing that is gossiped about, so they watch what their podcaster's and friends talk about. Or maybe they want to look smart, so they watch their favorite director's favorite director's neo-noir films. Or the IMDB top 250... same reasons.

My point here is just that people have many different reasons for watching a given thing on any given day. Let's hold onto that for later.

## What do I care most about as a streaming platform?

Making money.

How do I do this? Optimize the 50 options you see in such a way that is most profitable for me.

At this point, the streaming platform can insert the argument: "Viewers pick what they like, we give them more of what they like, that is all". This to me is the same sort of argument that a health insurance company will make: "We make money when our members spend less on healthcare, so we have aligned interests in negotiating down bills and encouraging our members to be healthy".

In healthcare, it is common knowledge that this is bullshit. It is clear that the health insurance companies are much more ready to make money from denying claims than they are making an effort to make healthcare cheaper or members healthier. Why is this? Denying claims is *far* easier than making someone healthy or negotiating with hospitals.

The same thing happens in media. Streamers (as the media middle men they are) have recognized that it is far easier to make mediocre content that will lull their viewers into watching and rewatching than it is to make bold and thought provoking material that may challenge their viewers.

So as the platform, I optimize for making money by making the lowest common denominator over and over so that subscribers stay on month after month.

## What do I care about as a media maker?

Film, Show, and Standup makers (and everyone else) have an interesting hodge-podge of incentives. Like the streamers, they have a top-line financial interest in the thing they are making. That is the foundation for which all future things will be made.

Different from the streamers though, these are artists. They want to make a thing that "says something", "means something", or just "has a place in the world". Given how much shit that these artists take in the entertainment industry, my guess is that the average maker here is an artist first and financially driven second. If the primary thing you value is money, this is not the job for you.

## Why Renting everything makes this worse

When we used to purchase films on dvd and blu-ray, this made it so that tv shows could only really exist on cable. The practicality of disks for tv shows was essentially zero. As a consequence, sitcoms ran supreme, they were the perfect recurring format that provided a balance of familiarity and novelty in each self-encapsulated episode. Streaming created the space for long-running character development in the way I believe HBO pioneered with The Sopranos, The Wire, and Twin Peaks on ABC.

But for movies, each one needed to sell you. They had a direct incentive to be the absolute best, all the time. You needed to buy each disk for the movie to be a hit. This was a direct viewer to maker connection.

Pooling movies on platforms waters each down to a point where it doesn't matter if any individual one is a hit, they just need to be the bare minimum that you don't cancel. And if Nestflix flops on a few, all they need to do as a holdover is promote some old hits and the viewers stay content. They do not care or want to make the best all the time.

HBO was making the best all the time, and we see how that ended now.

## Current State of the Media Cycle End-to-End

Currently, the movie makers make a thing, promote it direct-ish to their fans through social media channels, then hope their streamer-lords push the flick enough to get eyeballs on it. There's the whole theater thing too, but if you want to make something without explosions, good luck.

Anyways, the streamers are a true-to-form middle man. They obscure the feedback, charge for distribution and analytics, and finally exercise their power to shrink the media landscape to the minimal point of profitability. It is not in their interest to generate and promote variety.

This stifles makers and encourages them to fit into ever decreasing lanes through which the streamers can spoon feed their viewers. Viral Reality TV, Slam piece documentaries, fluffy feel good family films, and explosions (again).

If a maker wants to make something experimental there is seemingly no avenue for getting that out to the world unless they're a household name of a director.

## A better world

I believe there is zero reason beyond convenience that distribution should be at odds with the incentives of makers and viewers. There ought to be a world in which indy film makers, students, or fully proven directors can make whatever the fuck they want, then sell it directly to their fans.

More to come. In the meantime, tell your friends about what you're watching. Don't settle for the algorithm.
